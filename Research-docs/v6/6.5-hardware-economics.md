# Section 5: Hardware Economics and Micro-Quantization

## Executive Summary

The v5 plan's hard dependency on NVIDIA Blackwell B200 and NVFP4 format is **premature and unnecessarily risky** for an MVP. While NVFP4 on Blackwell delivers real throughput gains (1.59x over BF16), research reveals that NVFP4 "provably neutralizes traditional outlier mitigation techniques" and requires selective BF16 layers for convergence stability. Meanwhile, **H100/H200 with FP8 Transformer Engine achieves ~99% quality retention** at significantly lower rental cost (~$3/hr in 2026 vs ~$8-12/hr for B200). The recommended strategy: **develop and train MVP on H100/H200 + FP8**, design the architecture to be quantization-friendly, and migrate to B200 + NVFP4 for production scaling only after validating convergence.

## SOTA Analysis 2025-2026

### 5.1 NVFP4: Real Capabilities vs Marketing

**Confirmed benefits**:
- 4-bit E2M1 data format with per-block E4M3 scale factor
- Block size 16 (vs MXFP4's 32) → better dynamic range adaptation
- Two-level scaling: per-block scale + per-tensor scale
- 1.59x throughput over BF16 on Blackwell Tensor Cores
- Native hardware support via tcgen05 instructions

**Confirmed problems** (from independent research):

1. **Outlier neutralization**: "NVFP4's small group size provably neutralizes traditional outlier mitigation techniques" (Bridging the Gap, arxiv 2509.23202). Standard clipping/rotation methods that work for INT4 or MXFP4 fail for NVFP4.

2. **Selective BF16 requirement**: NVIDIA's own training recipe for NVFP4 requires keeping certain sensitive layers in BF16 to maintain convergence. For LLM training (Llama 3 8B), this means ~10-15% of layers remain in higher precision.

3. **MR-GPTQ necessity**: To achieve practical accuracy, block-wise Hadamard transforms and format-specific optimizations (MR-GPTQ) are needed, achieving up to 3.6x layer-wise speedups with proper accuracy.

4. **Not validated for 4DGS**: All published NVFP4 results are on language models (Llama, Mistral). The behavior of NVFP4 on geometric regression (predicting 3D coordinates, quaternions, SH coefficients) is **unknown and unvalidated**.

### 5.2 H100/H200 + FP8: The Pragmatic Alternative

**FP8 Transformer Engine (Hopper architecture)**:
- Native hardware support on H100/H200
- Two FP8 variants: E4M3 (higher precision) and E5M2 (wider range)
- Automatic mixed-precision: FP8 for compute, FP16/BF16 for master weights
- ~99% quality retention across LLM benchmarks
- 33% faster inference vs FP16
- 50% memory reduction for KV cache

**Market economics (2026)**:
| GPU | Rental cost ($/hr) | VRAM | Memory BW | FP8 TFLOPS |
|-----|-------------------|------|-----------|------------|
| H100 80GB | ~$2-3/hr | 80 GB | 3.35 TB/s | 1,979 |
| H200 141GB | ~$4-5/hr | 141 GB | 4.8 TB/s | 1,979 |
| B200 192GB | ~$8-12/hr | 192 GB | 8 TB/s | 4,500 (FP8) |

For MVP development (training runs of days-weeks), the cost difference is significant:
- 1 week H100 training: ~$500-700
- 1 week B200 training: ~$1,300-2,000
- **H100 is 2-3x cheaper per training run**

### 5.3 The 4DGS-Specific Quantization Challenge

4DGS parameters have unique quantization sensitivity:

| Parameter Group | Dimension | Sensitivity to quantization | Recommended precision |
|----------------|-----------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Position (μ) | 3 | **Very high** — sub-mm shifts visible | FP16 minimum |
| Quaternion (q) | 4 | **Very high** — small errors cause large rotations | FP16 minimum |
| Scale (s) | 3 | **High** — affects Gaussian footprint | FP8 acceptable |
| Opacity (α) | 1 | **Medium** — binary-like after training | FP8 or lower |
| SH DC (color) | 3 | **Medium** — smooth color space | FP8 acceptable |
| SH AC (high-freq) | 0 (if using MLP) | N/A | N/A (MLP weights) |
| DCT coefficients | 36 | **High** — trajectory errors accumulate | FP16 for first coefficients |

This heterogeneity means **uniform quantization is suboptimal**. A mixed-precision strategy per parameter group is needed regardless of hardware.

### 5.4 TMEM and Asynchronous Tensor Core Execution

Blackwell's TMEM (Tensor Memory) is genuinely innovative:
- Isolated memory pool for tensor core operands
- Eliminates register pressure at FP4 speeds
- Enables asynchronous execution: tensor cores compute while CUDA cores handle activation functions

This is a **real architectural advantage** that FP8 on Hopper cannot replicate. However, for our workload (dominated by attention mechanisms and rendering loss computation), the benefit is approximately 30-40% — meaningful but not transformational.

### 5.5 Hardware Decompression Engine

Blackwell's on-chip decompression (Snappy, LZ4, Deflate) is valuable for data loading from NVMe storage. However:
- Our 90-camera 4K data can be preprocessed and stored uncompressed in HBM
- The bottleneck for our workload is compute, not data loading
- nvCOMP library provides software decompression on Hopper with minimal CPU overhead

## Critical Analysis

### Risk 1: B200 Availability and Pricing Uncertainty

As of early 2026, B200 availability remains constrained for non-hyperscaler customers. Cloud providers prioritize large enterprises. A startup may face:
- 3-6 month wait for dedicated B200 instances
- Premium pricing during initial rollout period
- Potential for spot instance interruptions

H100/H200 is widely available with competitive pricing from multiple providers.

### Risk 2: NVFP4 Convergence for Geometric Regression

All published NVFP4 training results are on language models. The behavior of FP4 quantization on:
- 3D coordinate regression (continuous values in [-10, 10] range)
- Quaternion prediction (unit sphere constraint)
- Rendering loss gradients (flowing through differentiable rasterizer)

is **completely uncharacterized**. Training on B200 with NVFP4 could require significant hyperparameter tuning, selective layer precision, and custom quantization schedules — adding weeks to the development timeline.

### Risk 3: Vendor Lock-in

Hard dependency on Blackwell limits:
- Cloud provider options (fewer B200 instances available)
- Fallback strategies if training diverges
- Cost negotiation leverage

### Risk 4: Over-optimization at Wrong Stage

For MVP, the bottleneck is **algorithm correctness**, not hardware efficiency. Spending engineering time on NVFP4 optimization before validating the core architecture wastes resources.

## Architectural Recommendations for MVP

### Two-Phase Hardware Strategy

**Phase 1: MVP Development and Training (H100/H200 + FP8)**
- Use H100 80GB or H200 141GB instances
- FP8 Transformer Engine for all attention layers
- FP16 master weights for position and quaternion parameters
- Mixed precision: FP8 compute + FP16 accumulation
- Estimated cost: $15,000-25,000 for initial training campaign

**Phase 2: Production Scaling (B200 + NVFP4, optional)**
- Migrate to B200 only after architecture is validated
- Use NVFP4 for inference optimization
- Keep sensitive layers (position, quaternion heads) in FP8/FP16
- Estimated throughput gain: 1.5-2x over Phase 1

### Quantization-Friendly Architecture Design

Design the transformer architecture to be quantization-friendly from the start:

1. **Avoid extreme activation ranges**: Use LayerNorm before every attention block
2. **Smooth weight distributions**: Initialize with small fan-in variance
3. **Per-group quantization hooks**: Design parameter heads with grouped outputs so each group can have independent quantization scale
4. **Gradient checkpointing**: Reduce VRAM usage without quantization artifacts

### Cost Estimate

| Resource | Specification | Monthly cost |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Training cluster | 8× H100 80GB | ~$17,000/month |
| Storage | 50 TB NVMe | ~$2,000/month |
| Networking | 400 Gbps InfiniBand | Included |
| **Total MVP training** | **~3 months** | **~$57,000** |

vs B200 equivalent: ~$100,000+ for same training time.

## Open Questions

1. **FP8 vs BF16 for rendering loss**: Does the rendering loss (differentiable rasterization) degrade with FP8 activations? Requires benchmarking on gsplat with FP8 mode.
2. **Quantization-aware training**: Should we use QAT from the start, or train in FP16 first and quantize post-training?
3. **Multi-node scaling**: For 8+ GPU training, is NVLink (H100 DGX) necessary or can we use PCIe with gradient compression?
4. **B200 availability timeline**: When will B200 cloud instances be readily available at competitive pricing for startups?
